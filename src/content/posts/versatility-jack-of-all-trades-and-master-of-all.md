There's a saying in IT: jack of all trades, master of none. People use it like it's an insult. I don't take it that way.

Most of my career I've been the guy who gets called when something doesn't fit neatly into anyone else's lane. The networking issue that's actually an AD problem. The "email thing" that turns out to be a mail flow rule conflicting with a Sophos policy conflicting with a conditional access policy. The deployment that worked fine in the test environment and is now bricking machines across 48 clinic locations at 7am.

You don't get called for those problems if you only know one thing.

### The myth of the specialist

Specialization is valuable, I'm not arguing against it. But in most organizations, especially lean IT teams, MSPs, and mid-sized companies that can't afford a bench full of specialists, the person who can hold the whole stack in their head is worth more than three specialists who can't talk to each other.

I've worn a lot of hats. Helpdesk escalations, Windows infrastructure, M365 administration, network support, VoIP migrations, backup admin, endpoint imaging, security tooling, compliance coordination. Not because I couldn't pick a lane. Because the job needed it.

Somewhere along the way the breadth became the skill.

### What versatility actually looks like

It's not knowing everything. Nobody knows everything.

It's knowing enough about each layer that you can reason across all of them. When a user can't authenticate you're thinking about the endpoint, the network, Entra ID, the MFA policy, and whether something changed in the last 24 hours. All at once, not one at a time.

It's also knowing when you're out of your depth and who to call. Versatility isn't ego. It's map awareness.

### The master of all part

Here's the part of that quote people always leave off: jack of all trades, master of none, but often times better than a master of one.

The original quote wasn't an insult. It was an observation.

I'm not going to sit here and claim I'm the best at any single thing on my resume. But I shipped a 1,200 machine Windows 11 migration across 48 medical sites without touching clinical operations. I found $100K in unused M365 licensing in my first four months. I built a deployment pipeline that cut provisioning time by 87%.

That didn't come from specialization. It came from being able to see the whole board.

### So is it possible?

Yeah. I think so.

The ceiling for a generalist is high if you keep learning. The trap is coasting on breadth and never actually going deep on anything. The goal isn't to know a little about everything forever. It's to move the depth around based on what the work needs.

Right now that's Azure, automation, and infrastructure as code. Next year it'll be something else. That's just the job.
